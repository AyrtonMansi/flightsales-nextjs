## 19 First Avenue (Freddie / Widegate)

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This briefing package documents a pattern of conduct by Freddie Sheene (via Widegate or related entities) regarding the 19 First Avenue high-rise development that gives rise to significant legal claims including breach of shareholder duties, oppressive conduct, interference with corporate operations, and potentially tortious interference with business relations.

**Key Finding:** Freddie has engaged in a documented pattern of contradictory positions, issuing threats to force project liquidation while simultaneously claiming entitlement to 50% of profits—a position legally untenable given his refusal to contribute further capital, his attempts to force a sale, and his documented awareness of and participation in the project structure.

**Immediate Risk:** The ongoing threats of liquidation and interference with financing arrangements pose existential risks to the project that may justify urgent injunctive relief.

## PART 2 — KEY LEGAL ISSUES

### Issue 1: Breach of Shareholder/Partnership Duties

**What Occurred:**
- Freddie has alternately demanded equal profit share (50%), requested full buyout, and threatened project liquidation
- He has refused to contribute additional capital while demanding equal returns
- He has actively interfered with financing arrangements through threats and ultimatums

**Why It Matters Legally:**
- Parties in joint ventures owe duties of good faith and fair dealing
- Freddie's pattern of contradictory positions—claiming 50% while refusing obligations and threatening destruction of value—constitutes breach of fiduciary duty
- His documented awareness of and participation in financing strategies (Jan-Nov 2024) undermines any claim of exclusion

**Supporting Evidence:**
- WhatsApp 23 Jan 2024: Freddie encouraged mezzanine funding
- WhatsApp 22 Oct 2024: Active questioning of term sheets
- iMessage Dec 2024: "I'll ring MA myself and explain the whole situation... Cause you give me no information on anything" — admitting full knowledge

### Issue 2: Oppressive Conduct (Corporations Act or Partnership Analog)

**What Occurred:**
- Freddie's ultimatums and threats constitute oppressive conduct designed to force compliance or project termination
- Pattern: Threaten sale → Demand documents → Threaten legal action → Demand 50%

**Why It Matters Legally:**
- Oppressive conduct provisions (s 232-234 Corporations Act) or partnership analogs protect against conduct that is commercially unfair or prejudicial
- The documented pattern of threats and ultimatums, particularly during critical financing phases, is commercially oppressive

**Supporting Evidence:**
- WhatsApp 12 Nov 2024: "This is going to end up legal if I don't get this information"
- WhatsApp 18 Nov 2024: "It's either 50:50 or we are selling the site"
- iMessage Late Nov 2024: "You either do this MA deal or we are selling"

### Issue 3: Interference with Company Operations

**What Occurred:**
- Freddie's threats have directly interfered with financing negotiations and project completion
- His ultimatums have created uncertainty that jeopardizes lender relationships

**Why It Matters Legally:**
- Tortious interference with business relations requires: (1) intentional inducement, (2) breach of contract/expectation, (3) resulting damage
- Freddie's conduct may qualify as intentional interference causing project delays and financing costs

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Chronology shows repeated interference during critical financing phases (Oct-Nov 2024, Jan 2025)
- Threats to "ring MA myself" constitute direct interference with financing relationships

### Issue 4: Breach of Good Faith in Commercial Negotiations

**What Occurred:**
- Freddie's simultaneous demands for 50% profit share, offers to exit, and threats to sell/liquidate are fundamentally inconsistent
- His position changes based on tactical advantage, not principle

**Why It Matters Legally:**
- Australian contract and equity law imposes duties of good faith in commercial dealings
- Freddie's documented contradictions support inference of bad faith

## PART 5 — NEGOTIATION & SETTLEMENT PATH

### Option 1: Structured Buyout

**Structure:** Freddie receives buyout payment in exchange for:
- Transfer of all interests in 19 First Avenue project entities
- Release of all claims related to project
- Standstill agreement preventing interference

**Advantages:** Clean separation; removes source of interference; allows project to proceed

**Challenges:** Valuation disagreement; Freddie may demand premium; financing required

### Option 2: Profit Participation with Reduced Role

**Structure:** Freddie retains profit share (likely <50% given conduct) but:
- No further capital contribution obligations
- No voting rights on financing/construction decisions
- Non-interference covenants

**Advantages:** Lower immediate cash requirement; preserves some relationship

**Challenges:** Ongoing entanglement; Freddie may continue interference; difficult to enforce non-interference

### Option 3: Sell Project (Freddie's Preferred Outcome)

**Structure:** Market sale of 19 First Avenue with proceeds split

**Advantages:** Resolves dispute; immediate liquidity; eliminates ongoing conflict

**Challenges:** Likely lower value than development completion; Freddie has pushed for this, suggesting it may be favorable to him; difficult to achieve fair price under duress

### Option 4: Litigation to Judgment

**Structure:** Pursue claims for breach of duties, oppressive conduct, and damages

**Advantages:** Potential for damages award; court-ordered separation; precedent value

**Challenges:** Expensive; time-consuming; uncertain outcome; may further delay project

### Recommended Approach

**Phase 1:** Immediate injunctive relief to prevent liquidation/sale and stop interference
**Phase 2:** Parallel negotiation for structured buyout (Option 1)
**Phase 3:** If negotiation fails, proceed to trial on merits

## PART 7 — CRITICAL FACTS SUMMARY

### Project Background

The 19 First Avenue project is a high-rise residential development in Surfers Paradise. The project involves acquisition, DA approval, construction, and sale of residential units. Freddie Sheene and Ayrton Mansi entered into a joint arrangement for the project's development.

### Relationship Structure

The precise legal structure requires clarification (see Questions section). Based on communications, the parties appear to have a partnership or joint venture arrangement regarding the project, with Freddie holding some form of equity or profit participation.

### Development of the Dispute

The dispute emerged as the project required additional financing to proceed from acquisition to construction. Key phases:

1. **Early 2024:** Freddie actively participated in financing discussions, encouraging mezzanine funding approaches.

2. **Mid-2024:** As financing complexity increased, Freddie began expressing preference for sale over development.

3. **October-November 2024:** During critical MA Financial refinancing negotiations, Freddie engaged in pattern of threats and ultimatums.

4. **Late 2024-Early 2025:** Freddie alternated between demanding 50% profit share, offering to exit, and threatening liquidation.

5. **January 2026:** Recent text messages confirm ongoing pattern of coercive conduct.

### Key Turning Points

1. **November 18, 2024:** Freddie's ultimatum "It's either 50:50 or we are selling the site" marked escalation from negotiation to coercion.

2. **December 2024:** Freddie's admission "I'll ring MA myself and explain the whole situation" confirms his full awareness of financing arrangements, undermining any claim of exclusion.

3. **January 2026:** Recent threats indicate dispute remains active and Freddie continues coercive conduct.

### Why Freddie's Conduct Is Problematic

1. **Fundamental Contradiction:** Freddie cannot simultaneously demand 50% profit share, refuse to contribute capital, threaten liquidation, and claim to be excluded from decisions.

2. **Coercive Pattern:** The documented ultimatums—tying financing completion to threat of sale, threatening legal action if documents not provided—constitute bad faith pressure tactics.

3. **Destruction of Value:** Freddie's preferred outcome (sale) likely destroys significant development value compared to completion, harming both parties.

4. **Interference with Financing:** The pattern of threats during critical financing phases has likely increased project costs and may have permanently damaged lender relationships.
